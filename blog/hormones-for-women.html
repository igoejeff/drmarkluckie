The Truth About
Hormones for Women:
Beyond Hot Flashes

Dr. Mark Luckie, DCThe Performance Doctor — DC, Author & Speaker
January 28, 2025 9 min read Women's Health

For decades, the conversation about women's hormones has been dominated by one word: menopause. Hot flashes, night sweats, mood swings — these are the symptoms that finally drive women to seek help, usually in their late 40s or 50s. But here's what most doctors don't tell you: hormonal imbalance doesn't start at menopause. It starts in your 30s, sometimes even your late 20s. And the consequences go far beyond comfort — they reach directly into your energy, brain function, heart health, bone density, libido, and your quality of life every single day.

The Three Hormones Every Woman Needs to Understand

Women's hormonal health is an orchestra of interplaying hormones, but three conductors lead the entire performance:

Estrogen

The primary female sex hormone — but also a critical regulator of bone density, cardiovascular health, skin collagen, brain function, and mood. There are three types (estradiol, estrone, estriol), and the ratio between them matters enormously.

Progesterone

The calming, protective counterbalance to estrogen. Progesterone supports sleep quality, reduces anxiety, protects the uterine lining, and acts as a natural anti-inflammatory. Its decline in perimenopause is often the first domino to fall.

Testosterone

Yes, women have testosterone too — and need it. Women produce testosterone in the ovaries and adrenal glands. It drives libido, energy, lean muscle, bone density, mood, and cognitive sharpness. Low testosterone in women is massively under-diagnosed.

The Perimenopause Nobody Talks About

Perimenopause is the hormonal transition period before menopause — and it can start as early as 35. This is when progesterone begins its decline, estrogen becomes erratic (not just low, but wildly fluctuating), and testosterone quietly drops below optimal range. The result is a constellation of symptoms that most women are told is "stress" or "normal" or "just part of life."

It isn't normal. And it isn't inevitable. But it requires understanding, testing, and proactive treatment — not dismissal.

The Critical Window: The 5-10 years before menopause (perimenopause) are when hormonal intervention has the greatest protective benefit for the brain, heart, and bones. Acting early — not waiting until menopause — changes long-term outcomes dramatically.

Testosterone: The Woman's Forgotten Hormone

Ask most women about testosterone and they'll think it's a "man thing." In reality, women need testosterone just as much as men — just in smaller quantities. And the consequences of low testosterone in women are profound and consistently overlooked.

Signs of low testosterone in women:

  • Chronically low libido — even with a loving, attentive partner
  • Persistent fatigue that doesn't respond to sleep
  • Loss of muscle tone and strength despite working out
  • Increased body fat, especially visceral (belly) fat
  • Depression and loss of motivation
  • Cognitive fog and difficulty concentrating
  • Bone loss (osteopenia/osteoporosis)
  • Loss of competitive drive and confidence

Testosterone therapy in women — properly dosed and monitored — has been shown in multiple studies to restore libido, improve body composition, enhance mood, sharpen cognition, and protect bone and cardiovascular health. The key word is "properly" — this requires a physician who specializes in hormonal health, not a one-size-fits-all prescription.

The 15 Symptoms of Hormonal Imbalance in Women

01

Irregular or painful periods

The first sign that your hormonal cycle is disrupted — often indicating estrogen dominance or low progesterone.

02

Unexplained weight gain

Especially around the middle — driven by insulin resistance, cortisol, and declining estrogen and testosterone.

03

Hair thinning or loss

Both low and high androgens can cause hair changes. Thyroid dysfunction — closely tied to hormonal health — is also a major factor.

04

Skin changes: acne, dryness, loss of elasticity

Estrogen is directly responsible for collagen production. Declining estrogen accelerates skin aging dramatically.

05

Mood swings, anxiety, depression

Estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone all modulate neurotransmitters including serotonin, dopamine, and GABA.

06

Sleep disturbances

Low progesterone dramatically impairs sleep quality. Night sweats — caused by estrogen fluctuation — fragment the deepest restorative stages of sleep.

07

Brain fog and memory issues

Estrogen has profound neuroprotective effects. Its decline is directly associated with cognitive changes women often write off as "just getting older."

08

Hot flashes and night sweats

The classic signs of estrogen dysregulation — but representing just the tip of the iceberg of what hormonal imbalance is doing to the body.

09

Low libido and vaginal dryness

Both are direct consequences of declining estrogen and testosterone. Treatable, normalized, and nobody should simply "accept" them.

10

Fatigue unresponsive to sleep

Adrenal fatigue, thyroid issues, and sex hormone imbalances all converge to create this pervasive, frustrating exhaustion.

11

Bone pain or early osteoporosis

Estrogen and testosterone are critical for bone mineral density. Their decline accelerates bone loss dramatically after 40.

12

Digestive issues

Estrogen receptors line the gut. Hormonal changes alter the gut microbiome, slow motility, and increase inflammatory bowel symptoms.

13

Heart palpitations

Estrogen is cardioprotective. Its loss removes key protections and can cause sensations of racing heart or irregular beats — especially at night.

14

Headaches and migraines

Especially around the menstrual cycle — driven by estrogen fluctuations that affect brain blood vessel tone and serotonin levels.

15

Loss of confidence and motivation

The psychological impact of hormonal decline is real and biological — not weakness. Addressing the root cause transforms this entirely.

HRT for Women: The Science vs. The Fear

The 2002 Women's Health Initiative study created a generation of fear around hormone replacement therapy for women. That fear was largely misplaced — based on a poorly designed study using synthetic, non-bioidentical hormones in older women who were already past their optimal treatment window. The science has moved dramatically since then.

Current evidence from the Menopause Society, multiple long-term studies, and the Lancet clearly shows that bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (BHRT), when started early in perimenopause or at menopause, provides significant protective benefits for the brain, heart, and bones — with risks that are minimal when properly managed.

The key distinctions that matter:

  • Bioidentical vs. synthetic: Bioidentical hormones structurally match what your body produces. Synthetic versions (like Provera) do not — and carry different risk profiles.
  • Timing matters: Starting HRT within 10 years of menopause onset (the "timing hypothesis") dramatically changes the risk-benefit calculus
  • Route of delivery: Transdermal (patches, gels, creams) estrogen bypasses first-pass liver metabolism and carries significantly lower blood clot risk than oral estrogen
  • Individualization: Cookie-cutter HRT is the wrong approach. Every woman deserves a protocol tailored to her specific hormonal profile, symptoms, family history, and goals

Natural Hormonal Support Strategies

Whether or not you pursue HRT, these lifestyle strategies powerfully support hormonal health at any age:

1

Prioritize Sleep Above Everything Else

Growth hormone and progesterone are primarily secreted during deep sleep. Chronic sleep deprivation creates a hormonal deficit that no supplement or pill can fully overcome.

2

Resistance Training 3x Per Week

Lifting weights is the most effective natural intervention for testosterone and growth hormone in women. It also builds the muscle and bone density that protect against aging.

3

Reduce Xenoestrogens

BPA from plastics, parabens in personal care products, and pesticides mimic estrogen and disrupt hormonal signaling. Switch to glass containers, natural personal care products, and organic produce.

4

Manage Cortisol Aggressively

Chronic stress causes "cortisol steal" — where the building blocks for sex hormones are diverted to make cortisol instead. Meditation, yoga, breathwork, and therapy are not luxuries — they're hormonal medicine.

5

Support Estrogen Metabolism with Cruciferous Vegetables

Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, and kale contain DIM (diindolylmethane), which helps the body metabolize estrogen safely and reduce the risk of estrogen dominance.

What to Ask Your Doctor to Test

If you suspect hormonal imbalance, advocate for a comprehensive hormonal panel that includes:

  • Estradiol (E2) and Estrone (E1) — not just "estrogen"
  • Progesterone — timed to the appropriate phase of your cycle
  • Total and Free Testosterone — critical and frequently overlooked in women
  • DHEA-S — the precursor to sex hormones and a marker of adrenal health
  • TSH, Free T3, Free T4 — thyroid hormones are intimately connected to sex hormone balance
  • Cortisol (4-point saliva test) — to assess adrenal function and daily cortisol pattern
  • Fasting insulin and HbA1c — insulin resistance dramatically worsens hormonal imbalance
  • hsCRP — inflammation marker that reflects overall hormonal health status
